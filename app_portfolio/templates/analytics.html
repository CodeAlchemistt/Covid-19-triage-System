THE DATASET

The dataset originates from the Mexican Secretariat of Health (Secretaría de Salud) — Mexico's open COVID-19 surveillance data, covering confirmed and suspected cases from 2020 to 2022.

It contains 1,048,576 patient records with 21 clinical variables per patient, including demographic information, institution type, comorbidity flags, and — critically — the patient outcome (survived vs. died), which forms the prediction target.

Unlike many academic COVID datasets, this data reflects real triage decisions made by frontline medical staff under crisis conditions, making it both highly representative and inherently noisy.

THE PROBLEM

Only ~9.6% of records in the dataset have a mortality outcome. A naive model that predicts "survived" for every patient would achieve 90.4% accuracy — while detecting zero deaths.

THE SOLUTION: SMOTE

Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) was applied exclusively to the training split (never the test set). SMOTE generates synthetic minority-class examples by interpolating between existing minority samples in feature space, forcing the model to learn meaningful decision boundaries.

EVALUATION METRICS

Raw accuracy was discarded as the primary metric in favour of Precision, Recall, F1-Score, and AUC-ROC — all of which capture model behaviour on the minority class. A high recall is particularly valued to minimise missed critical cases.

KEY INSIGHT

Age and Pneumonia are the strongest predictors, reflecting clinical consensus. The model's top 4 features (Age, Pneumonia, Patient Type, Diabetes) together account for over 58% of total importance weight — a result consistent across multiple cross-validation folds.

Notably, INTUBED and ICU rank highly due to their direct clinical relationship with severity outcomes, though they represent concurrent observations rather than predictive features. They are included for completeness but can be excluded in a purely predictive deployment.

METHODOLOGY

STEP 01

DATA ACQUISITION & EDA

Downloaded the SSA COVID-19 dataset as a CSV. Loaded into Pandas for initial exploration: null counts (2–3% on comorbidity fields), data type validation, and class distribution visualisation. Identified the 1:9.4 class imbalance. Plotted age distributions by outcome and correlation heatmaps for comorbidity features.

pandas matplotlib seaborn

STEP 02

PREPROCESSING & ENCODING

Missing values in comorbidity columns (coded as 97/98/99 in the raw data) were recoded as 0 (not reported). Binary categorical features were left as-is. The target variable (FECHA_DEF — date of death) was binarised: deceased = 1, survived = 0. 80/20 stratified train/test split applied.

sklearn numpy train_test_split

STEP 03

SMOTE OVERSAMPLING

SMOTE was applied only to the training set after splitting to prevent data leakage into the test evaluation. Post-SMOTE, the training set became balanced at 1:1 (survived : deceased), enabling the Random Forest to learn genuine minority-class patterns.

imblearn SMOTE

STEP 04

TRAINING & TUNING

RandomForestClassifier trained with n_estimators=500, max_depth=None, class_weight='balanced' as a secondary safeguard. GridSearchCV with 5-fold CV optimised max_features and min_samples_split. Final model serialised with joblib for Flask API deployment.

RandomForestClassifier GridSearchCV joblib

STEP 05

FLASK API & DEPLOYMENT

The trained model and preprocessing pipeline are loaded at startup into a Flask application. The /api/predict_risk endpoint accepts a JSON payload, preprocesses the 21 features, calls model.predict_proba(), and returns the risk score along with the STABLE / CRITICAL verdict.
